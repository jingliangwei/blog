## Radiation Fundamentals

### Brightness and Flux Density

- Specific intensity / brightness $I_\nu$ or $I_\lambda$
    $$
    I_\nu\equiv\frac{\mathrm{d}P}{(\,\cos\theta\ \mathrm{d}\sigma\,)\ \mathrm{d}\nu\ \mathrm{d}\Omega}
    $$
    $$
    I_\lambda\equiv\frac{\mathrm{d}P}{(\,\cos\theta\ \mathrm{d}\sigma\,)\ \mathrm{d}\lambda\ \mathrm{d}\Omega}
    $$

::: info units
- MKS units: metre, kilogram, second
- cgs units: centimetre, gram, second
:::

- Total intensity $I$
    $$
    I\equiv\int_0^\infty I_\nu(\nu)\,\mathrm{d}\nu=\int_0^\infty I_\lambda(\lambda)\,\mathrm{d}\lambda
    $$

- Flux density $S_\nu$
    $$
    S_\nu=\int_{\text{source}} I_\nu(\theta,\phi)\cos\theta\,\mathrm{d}\Omega
    $$
  astronomers use smaller units
  $$
  1\ \text{jansky}=1\ \text{Jy}\equiv 10^{-26} \text{ W m}^{-2}\text{ Hz}^{-1}
  $$

- Spectral luminosity $L_\nu$
    $$
    L_\nu=4\pi d^2 S_\nu
    $$

- Luminosity $L$
    $$
    L\equiv \int_0^\infty L_\nu\,\mathrm{d}\nu
    $$

### Radiative Transfer

1. Absorption

    - the linear absorption coefficient $\kappa$
        $$
        \frac{\mathrm{d}I_\nu}{I_\nu}=-\kappa\ \mathrm{d}s
        $$
    - the optical depth or opacity $\tau$
        $$
        \tau\equiv-\int_{s_{\text{out}}}^{s_{\text{in}}}\kappa(s')\,\mathrm{d}s'
        $$
    
      thus

        $$
        \frac{I_\nu(s_{\text{out}})}{I_\nu(s_{\text{in}})}=\exp(-\tau)
        $$

      If $\tau\ll 1$, the absorber is said to be optically thin; if $\tau\gg 1$, it is optically thick.

2. Emission

    - the emission coefficient $j_v$
        $$
        j_\nu\equiv\frac{\mathrm{d}I_\nu}{\mathrm{d}s}
        $$
    - the equation of radiative transfer
        $$
        \frac{\mathrm{d}I_\nu}{\mathrm{d}s}=-\kappa I_\nu+j_\nu
        $$

In fact, the absorption coefficient and emission coefficient is not independent in full **thermodynamic equilibrium (TE)**

- Kirchhoff's law for a system in TE:
    $$
    \frac{j_\nu(T)}{\kappa(T)}=B_\nu(T)
    $$

  where $B_\nu(T)$ is the blackbody radiation. It applies in **local thermodynamic equilibrium (LTE)** as well as in TE.

### Emission and Absorption of Radio Waves in the Earth's Atmosphere

- brightness temperature $T_b$
    $$
    T_b(\nu)\equiv\frac{I_\nu c^2}{2k\nu^2}
    $$

Using Kirchhoff's law to determine atmospheric absorption by measuring atmospheric emission as a function of zenith angle.

$$
\left\{\begin{array}{l}
\dfrac{\mathrm{d}I_\nu}{\mathrm{d}s}=-\kappa I_\nu+j_\nu \\
j_\nu=\kappa B_\nu(T_{\text{atm}})
\end{array}\right.\Rightarrow
$$
$$
T_b=T_{\text{atm}}[1-\exp(-\tau_Z\sec z)]
$$

### Emission, Absorption, and Reflection from an Opaque Body

- absorption coefficient $a(\nu)$
- reflection coefficient $r(\nu)$
- opaque: $a(\nu)+r(\nu)=1$
- emission coefficient $e(\nu)$
- Kirchhoff's law for opaque bodies: $e(\nu)=a(\nu)=1-r(\nu)$

### Blackbody Radiation

blackbody: $a(\nu)=1$

#### The Rayleigh–Jeans Approximation

1. standing waves
    $$
    \nu=\frac{c}{2a}\sqrt{n_x^2+n_y^2+n_z^2}
    $$
    modes' numbers
    $$
    N_\nu(\nu)\mathrm{d}\nu=\frac{4\pi\rho^2\mathrm{d}\rho}{8}\times 2=\pi\left(\frac{2a\nu}{c}\right)^2\frac{2a}{c}\mathrm{d}\nu
    $$

2. the average energy per mode
    $$
    \langle E\rangle=\frac{\int_0^\infty EP(E)\mathrm{d}E}{\int_0^\infty P(E)\mathrm{d}E}=kT,\quad P(E)\propto\exp\left(-\frac{E}{kT}\right)
    $$

3. the spectral energy density
    $$
    u_\nu=\frac{1}{c}\int_{4\pi}B_\nu\mathrm{d}\Omega=\frac{4\pi}{c}B_\nu
    $$

4. the Rayleigh-Jeans approximation
$$
B_\nu=\frac{2kT\nu^2}{c^2}=\frac{2kT}{\lambda^2}
$$
that is valid only in the low-frequency limit $h\nu\ll kT$

#### The Planck Radiation Law

1. obtain the Planck's law:
$$
P(E)=P(nh\nu)\propto\exp\left(-\frac{nh\nu}{kT}\right)
$$
$$
\langle E\rangle=\frac{\sum_{n=0}^\infty nh\nu P(nh\nu)}{\sum_{n=0}^\infty P(nh\nu)}=\frac{h\nu}{\displaystyle\exp\left(\frac{h\nu}{kT}\right)-1}
$$
$$
B_\nu=\frac{2kT\nu^2}{c^2}\left[\frac{\displaystyle\frac{h\nu}{kT}}{\displaystyle\exp\left(\frac{h\nu}{kT}\right)-1}\right]=\frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2}\frac{1}{\displaystyle \exp\left(\frac{h\nu}{kT}\right)-1}
$$

2. Stefan-Boltzmann law
$$
B(T)\equiv\int_0^\infty B_\nu(T)\mathrm{d}\nu=\frac{\sigma T^4}{\pi}
$$
Stefan-Boltzmann constant
$$
\sigma\equiv\frac{2\pi^5 k^4}{15 c^2 h^3}\approx 5.67\times 10^{-5}\frac{\text{erg}}{\text{cm}^2\text{ s K}^4\text{ (sr)}}
$$

3. "Wien's displacement law"
$$
\left(\frac{\lambda_{\text{max}}}{\text{cm}}\right)\approx 0.29\left(\frac{T}{\text{K}}\right)^{-1}
$$
familiar form in optical one, but not the same, here use brightness.

### Noise Generated by a Warm Resistor

- Nyquist formula
$$
P_\nu=\frac{h\nu}{\exp\left(\dfrac{h\nu}{kT}\right)-1}
